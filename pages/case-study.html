The Firm

A well-regarded practice that had quietly outgrown the way it ran.

This firm had been operating for over a decade. Three principals across two offices, supported by six staff — a mix of full and part-time. The work was good. The client relationships were strong. The firm had grown steadily, largely on reputation.

But somewhere in the growth, the operational foundations hadn't kept pace. The principals were carrying more than they should have — not because the team wasn't capable, but because the systems around them weren't designed to carry the load.

The managing principal put it plainly at the outset: "I spend more time managing the firm than running it. I'm not sure when that happened."

What Practice Flow Found

Four friction points. Each one costing more than the firm realised.

01

Work entered the firm inconsistently

New client intake varied by principal. Support staff never knew what to expect or what was needed next. Files sat incomplete because nobody had defined what complete looked like — so the principals filled the gap, every time.

02

The practice management system wasn't trusted

LEAP held the data but not the team's confidence. Parallel records — emails, spreadsheets, handwritten notes — had grown up around it. The system was being used as a filing cabinet, not a workflow tool. Information was findable, but only if you knew where to look.

03

Support staff were capable but directionless

When a principal was available, work moved. When they were in court or with a client, it didn't. The team did their best work reactively — responding to what was asked of them — because the process for doing things proactively hadn't been built.

04

Departures were expensive and disruptive

Two support staff had left in eighteen months. Each departure cost in advertising, principal time, and a slow ramp-up — because there was no documented process for a new person to follow. The firm absorbed all of it, quietly, without ever adding it up.

The Practice Flow Engagement

Six months. Highest friction first.

Month 1–2

Standardised client intake across both offices

A consistent intake process was designed, documented, and embedded — the same regardless of which principal opened the matter or which office handled it. For the first time, support staff knew exactly what was needed before they had to ask.

Month 2–3

LEAP reconfigured to reflect how the firm actually works

Matter templates were rebuilt. Status fields were mapped to real workflow stages. The team was trained — not on the software, but on the process the software now supported. The parallel records disappeared within weeks, because the system had finally earned their trust.

Month 3–4

AI introduced for routine correspondence and document review

First-pass document review and client update drafts were handed to AI tools — reducing the time principals spent on routine correspondence by an estimated three hours per week. The principals reviewed and approved. The drafting stopped being their job.

Month 4–5

Communication structure established across both offices

A clear protocol replaced the tangle of email threads and ad hoc conversations. Support staff could follow it without needing to ask. Information reached the right people. The principals stopped being the only reliable path for anything important.

Month 5–6

Staff onboarding guide built and embedded

A documented onboarding process was built for new support staff — drawing on how the firm actually worked, not how a generic manual said it should. Ramp-up time reduced from approximately three months to four weeks. The process doesn't leave when a person does.

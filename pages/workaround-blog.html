The Workaround That Became Permanent

There's a workaround in your law firm that's been there so long nobody remembers why it started. It didn't begin as a workaround. It began as a solution.

Maybe a partner left and someone inherited their files without a handover. Maybe the billing software couldn't do what you needed, so someone built a spreadsheet to fill the gap. Maybe a client had an unusual requirement that became, over time, just the way things are done. Whatever the origin, the patch held. And because it held, nobody touched it.

That's how workarounds survive. Not because they work well — because they work well enough, and changing them feels riskier than leaving them alone. There's institutional memory locked inside them. There's someone who knows the steps, even if they've never written them down. There's a quiet understanding among the people who touch it: don't ask too many questions.

The cost doesn't show up on a balance sheet. It shows up in the extra thirty minutes it takes to prepare a matter file.

The cost doesn't show up on a balance sheet. It shows up in the extra thirty minutes it takes to prepare a matter file. In the associate who re-enters the same information in three different places because the systems don't talk to each other. In the partner who keeps a separate tracking sheet because she doesn't trust the official one. In the onboarding that takes twice as long as it should because the process only lives in one person's head.

None of these feel like crises. That's the problem. They feel like the job.

The firms that run well aren't the ones that have eliminated all complexity — legal work is complex by nature. They're the ones that can distinguish between complexity that's inherent to the work and complexity that's been accidentally engineered into how the work gets done. Those are very different things, and they have very different solutions.

When you look clearly at how your firm actually operates — not how it's supposed to operate, not how it operated five years ago — you start to see the workarounds for what they are: decisions made under pressure that never got revisited. Most of them made sense once. Some of them still do. But a surprising number are just friction that everyone has quietly agreed to live with.

The question isn't whether your firm has workarounds. Every firm does. The question is whether you're running them, or they're running you.
